# The Collector: What 2,400 Turns of Empo Actually Show

*Player Study Program · Profile #1 · Draft for publication — community-voice section pending*

---

There's a moment in the OST XVII finals decider — [turn 119 of 144](https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen8ou-551299) — where Empo's Heatran catches Lord_Enz's Corviknight in Magma Storm, and the whole game folds open. That Corviknight had Defogged Empo's Stealth Rock nine times. Empo had clicked it twelve. For a hundred and eighteen turns the two of them had been passing the same coin back and forth, and you could be forgiven for thinking nothing was happening. Then the trap closes, Earth Power lands, the storm's residual finishes the job, the Defogger is gone — and suddenly the rocks are permanent, the Toxic that finally stuck on turn 97 is a countdown, and twenty-four turns later Hydreigon drops to a Clefable Moonblast and the series is over. Empo won a 144-turn title decider without losing a single Pokémon.

For a hundred and eighteen turns, he'd been collecting the change.

This is the first profile in a series studying sixteen Hall of Fame players from their full tournament-replay corpora — no highlight reels, no memory, just what the logs show. Empo goes first because his résumé spans the widest arc: per the program's discovery career map, three Smogon Tour trophies (XXX, XXXIII, XXXVI), OST XVII, Masters II, two SPLs, two World Cups, and the #1 slot in the 2026 Hall of Fame revision. The archive gave us 50 verifiable games across Gens 6–9 — playoff-heavy, 27 elimination games, three complete finals series — and every result below is read from the replay's own `|win|` line. (Method, caveats, and the full numbers live in the appendix. The short version: this is a corpus, not a career; treat the 39–11 record as a property of what got archived.)

Here is what the corpus says. Some of it you will have guessed. Some of it we did not.

## His most-clicked attack is tied with a move that does 65 base power

Across 1,644 recorded moves, Empo's most-clicked attack is Moonblast, at 113. Tied with it, at exactly 113: **Knock Off.**

Sit with that. The best player of his era clicks item removal as often as he clicks his best attack. And the logs show it isn't filler — it's aimed. In [game two of those same OST finals](https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen8ou-551243), the decisive click of a 57-turn game comes on turn 44, when Mandibuzz comes in to Defog and Empo Knocks its Heavy-Duty Boots *on the Defog turn itself*. The removal turn is the one turn the remover can't fight back. After that, Mandibuzz takes rocks on every entry, the removal war is unwinnable, and — here's the detail that tells you what game Empo is actually playing — he doesn't take his first KO of the game until **turn 50**. He wins with all six of his Pokémon standing. The faints were never the score. They were the receipt.

The same instinct shows up everywhere once you look for it. In the decider he Tricks *twice in one game* — Choice Specs onto Toxapex on turn 56 to lock the wall, then steals Clefable's Leftovers on turn 72 with the now-itemless Blacephalon. In a [126-turn quarterfinal against Ox the Fox](https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen7ou-627209), Skarmory's Whirlwind — a move he clicked 23 times in this corpus — drags Excadrill into a bed of Spikes on turn 90 and simply deletes it. No attack ever touched it.

## He almost never sets up — and neither do you, when you play him

Strip the corpus down to stat-boosting moves and Empo clicks one **3.77%** of the time. His opponents, in the very same games, click them at **7.37%** — nearly double. That's not an artifact of slow teams: measured against those same opponents he's actually *more* direct (his non-attacking share is lower than theirs), and he clicks recovery *less* than they do — 10.6% against their 13.2%. Read that pair again. The grindiest player of his generation is not the one clicking Recover. He's the one making *you* click it.

What he invests in instead is the mirror image: infrastructure that deletes *your* boosts. Haze erases Magearna and Garchomp three times in [a finals win over SOULWIND](https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen8ou-533296). Whirlwind phazes a Calm Mind Reflect-Type Latias out five separate times across the Ox the Fox marathon. Clefable absorbs three Hydreigon Nasty Plots in the OST decider without blinking. Empo doesn't climb ladders. He cuts yours down and waits.

And the hazards — the one thing he does set — go down two turns earlier than the field's (median turn 3 against turn 5) and then get *re-bought* relentlessly: 159 hazard clicks in the corpus, nearly four per hazard game, recurring as deep as turn 123. Most players treat rocks as an opening chore. Empo treats them as a subscription he's happy to keep paying, because every Defog you click is a free turn he spends somewhere else.

## The number that should change how you think about winning

Here is the revelation the whole study bent around. In 19 of his 50 games, Empo never fell behind on Pokémon. He won **all nineteen**. In 23 games his worst deficit was exactly one Pokémon down — he won 19 of those, 82.6%. Two down, the cliff: one win in five. And every single one of his eleven losses passed through a deficit first.

The lazy reading is "front-runner." The logs refuse it. His opponents fell two-or-more behind in 82% of these games; Empo did in 16%. When they went two down against him, they came back 7% of the time — his own rate from the same hole is *higher*. Nobody climbs out of deep holes at this level. The skill isn't the climbing. **It's never being in the hole** — and the mechanism is visible on tape: his deficits are purchases, not accidents. Watch [the only two-down comeback in the corpus](https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen9ou-772080), a 26-turn SV quarterfinal against Santu. Slowking-Galar dies — but buys Thunder Wave on Kyurem. Darkrai dies — but buys Will-O-Wisp on Landorus. Weavile dies — but its final click Knocks Kingambit's Leftovers. Then Iron Defense Skarmory walks out and Body Presses its way through everything left standing — four consecutive KOs in five turns. That wasn't a comeback. That was a layaway plan.

The same accounting explains the eeriest stat in the file: when Empo draws first blood he converts it to a win 81% of the time — but when *opponents* draw first blood on him, they still lose 74% of the time. First blood isn't causing anything in these games. By the time the faints start, the structure has usually already decided them. His first KO arrives on turn 50 of one finals game, turn 33 of a 144-turn one. The scoreboard is a lagging indicator of a war you lost in the item slot.

## Don't call him a grinder — watch the Medicham game

If all of this paints a picture of a man who only wins slowly, [the Gen 7 finals game against SOULWIND](https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen7ou-533288) burns it down in eighteen turns. Mega Medicham clicks High Jump Kick four times in a row — into switches, through everything — and the game is over almost before it starts. Reckless? Look at team preview. SOULWIND's six contain **no Ghost-type**. The move that punishes HJK spam — the 50% crash into an immune switch-in — *does not exist in the game*. The most famous coin-flip move in the format was, on that day, not a coin flip, and Empo spammed it like a man reading from an answer key.

That's the profile's real spine, and it's the same one in the marathons: he suppresses variance ruthlessly (four Toxic misses and a flinch in the OST decider produced zero deviation from plan) and then buys it back only at verified prices. The aggression isn't a mood. It's a receipt.

## How he loses

Eleven losses, and they sort into exactly two shapes. The first: **out-engined**. Against [Santu's Future Sight/Teleport machine](https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen8ou-772055) he was ground down 0–6 over 115 turns — beaten at his own arithmetic. And in [the 191-turn epic against 1 True Lycan](https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen8ou-470762) — the longest game in the corpus — the adjudication corrected our own first draft: Empo didn't fail to shift gears, he clicked **fifteen Bulk Ups**, and his opponent answered with **ten Hazes**, erasing the work almost one-for-one. Under pressure his play measurably intensifies — switching rises, setup nearly triples — but it intensifies into *more of the same grind*. What he doesn't own is an offensive gear. When your engine is genuinely bigger than his, he has no eject button. The second shape is rarer and uglier: the **tempo gift** — [a 10-turn collapse](https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen8ou-473406) where a turn-1 Thunder Wave into a Dragon-Dancing Dragapult handed the game to a sweeper that never needed to switch.

One more thing the logs show about losing: he concedes *positions*, not Pokémon counts. He forfeited a finals game down only one with five Pokémon standing, and another down two with four alive — the moment the math died, so did the game. Twenty of his 39 wins end with the opponent's forfeit message; that courtesy runs both ways at this level. But when a branch still lived — one miss, one flinch of daylight — he played 191 turns without blinking.

## The SV question mark

Gen 9 gives us only four games, all wins, all short, and the honest answer is that four games prove nothing — the program treats it as an open question and is commissioning a top-up. What the four games *do* show: Tera held to turns 17 and 20 (or banked entirely — that Skarmory comeback never spent it), always as a terminal tool, never an opener; hazards outsourced to the mechanics themselves (Samurott's Ceaseless Edge lays Spikes by attacking; a dying Glimmora drops Toxic Spikes on its way out); and — the detail that argues continuity over reinvention — the Gen 8 Iron Defense/Body Press Skarmory win condition reappearing *verbatim* as an SV closer. The costume changed. The man underneath looks familiar.

## The label

The program scores every subject on six axes and then borrows a label from chess. Empo's card reads: variance-suppressing, counter-punch by default, accumulation to the bone, scout-patient, range across four generations and every archetype from Sticky Web offense to full stall. The chess word for that player — the one who never gives you a target, wins every exchange by a sliver, and turns your position into a slowly tightening arithmetic problem — is the **Python**.

Turn 144 of the decider, when Hydreigon finally falls, isn't a knockout punch. It's the last line of an invoice — the rocks re-bought twelve times, the Boots knocked on the Defog turn, the Toxic that took four tries to land, all of it itemized across a hundred and forty-four turns and presented, politely, for payment in full. Empo doesn't beat you. He *bills* you.

**[HIDE — Data appendix & method]**

**Corpus.** 54 archived replays from trophy-bearing officials, 2018–2024 by upload date → 50 confirmed-Empo games (4 wrong-player suspects excluded from all metrics). Formats: Gen 8 ×25, Gen 7 ×15, Gen 6 ×6, Gen 9 ×4. Results derived from log `|win|` lines (the discovery manifest's result fields were unusable: 48/54 unknown, one wrong). Record in-corpus: 39–11. Playoff-skewed by archive composition (Smogon Tour 23–3 vs WCoP 1–2 is discovery bias, not a circuit split); 76 known games have no surviving replay and one endpoint is dead — documented gaps, per program rules. Manifest dates are archive-thread dates (wrong in 53/54); only upload timestamps are used for chronology.

**Key figures (all log-computed, cross-verified by two independent analysis pipelines plus two external second opinions on the same raw corpus).** Moves n=1,644. Knock Off 113 · Moonblast 113 · Stealth Rock 94 · Spikes 56 · Toxic 44 · Body Press 30 · Whirlwind 23. Stat-raising setup 3.77% (opponents in the same games: 7.37%); setup 2.55% in wins vs 7.71% in losses. Recovery 10.6% vs opponents' 13.2%. Voluntary switches 0.275/turn (opponents 0.277) — rising in losses under all three measurement definitions. Hazards in 82% of games, median first hazard turn 3 (field: 5), 159 selections, mean 3.88 per hazard game. Leads: 25 distinct species in 50 games, normalized entropy >0.91 in every generation. Deficit table (max deficit → W–L): never behind 19–0 · one down 19–4 · two down 1–4 · three/four down 0–3. First blood drawn 54%, converted 81%; opponents' conversion against him 26%. Game length: median 33, mean 48.4, eight games ≥81 turns, max 191. Tera (n=4 games): activations at turns 17 and 20 plus one game with no activation; the fourth event is withheld here because it anchors a sealed training position in the research dossier.

**Method.** Symmetric per-side event parsing of raw Showdown protocol; opponent-population baseline computed from the identical games; twelve-game curated deep read (losses, outliers, elimination games, cross-gen pairs, games vs other study subjects); two external analyst briefs produced from the raw corpus (never from our draft) and adjudicated claim-by-claim against the logs — one external claim refuted, one of our own corrected, one axis honestly downgraded to "undecidable from logs." Full research dossier, scripts, and per-game index: `analysis/empo/` in the program repository. Every factual claim in the essay above traces to a cited game and turn in that dossier.
